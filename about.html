The Mice and the Machine

A Manifesto for the Pavlov Test—The Next Generation of AI Evaluation

Introduction

We stand on the cusp of a technological and biological revolution—a crossroads where artificial intelligence and animal cognition converge in a way that few could have imagined. The "Turing test" as we know it has grown stale.

Originally conceived by Alan Turing in 1950, the Turing test challenged machines to mimic human conversation so convincingly that a human judge could not reliably distinguish AI from human. It was revolutionary for its time, but in an era when Large Language Models (LLMs) can produce startlingly human-like text, the simple format of guessing games through a chat interface no longer feels like a sufficient measure.

The Pavlov Test: A New Paradigm

We propose a new standard we call the "Pavlov Test." Named in honor of Ivan Pavlov, who famously demonstrated the conditioning of dogs through stimuli and rewards, this test asks: can an AI, with no human intermediary, shape the behavior of another living organism through subtle environmental controls?

While Turing's measure dwelled in the realm of conversation, the Pavlov Test moves us into the domain of sensory-motor reality, focusing on whether an AI can understand, predict, and influence the actions of living beings—first mice, and ultimately more complex species.

From Turing's Chatroom to the Animal Habitat

The Turing test was a stunning leap forward in its day, pushing us to consider whether machines could "think" by simulating conversation. But modern LLMs have outgrown this benchmark. They can craft poetry, summarize dense academic papers, and write code. And yet, they remain trapped in a purely textual universe, devoid of real sensorimotor grounding.

The Pavlov Test sets a more ambitious challenge: the AI observes mice in a controlled environment, uses cues like sound and water rewards, and attempts to train them to perform actions—such as pressing a lever. Success demands not just clever wordplay, but understanding cause-and-effect in a living system.

The Importance of Domestication to Human Intelligence

Our ability to domesticate and interact with animals—dogs, horses, cattle—has been pivotal to human civilization and intelligence. This relationship influenced our cognitive evolution. Humans learned to control and predict the behavior of other species, leveraging animals for agriculture, transportation, and companionship.

By challenging AI to do something similarly complex—shaping another species' behavior—the Pavlov Test taps into a deep well of evolutionary intelligence. In a sense, we are asking AI to replicate a small slice of what made human cognition so successful: the subtle art of influencing and harmonizing with other living beings.

Evidence for the Next Generation of AI Testing

Research in embodied AI and robotics consistently shows that navigating the real world challenges machines far beyond what text can. Mice, unlike simulated agents, have unpredictable behaviors and internal motivations. Mastering this environment forces AI into real-time adaptation.

Traditional Turing-like tests rely on a single modality—language. The Pavlov Test demands integration of visual, auditory, and even tactile information through reward dispensing. This complexity better mirrors the full richness of human learning and intelligence.
